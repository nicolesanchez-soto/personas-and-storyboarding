From Tap to Pour: Understanding User Interactions with Soda Dispensers

It’s lunchtime at Andrews, and like many students, I find myself standing in line, cup in hand, waiting for my turn at the Coca-Cola Freestyle machine. The Coca-Cola Freestyle machine is a widely used public interface, found in dining halls, restaurants, and fast-food chains. What used to be a simple, almost thoughtless action—pressing a lever to fill my cup—has transformed into a digital experience. Now, getting a drink means tapping through a glowing touchscreen, scrolling through an overwhelming number of flavor options, and waiting for the machine to process my choice.

For some, this sleek, high-tech upgrade feels like progress—more variety, a modern interface, and even a satisfying vibration when you press the virtual button. For others, it’s just another barrier between them and a quick refreshment. As I inch forward in line, I can’t help but ask: Is this machine making things better, or is it complicating something that never needed fixing?

To find out, I observed and interviewed students using the machine. Was the interface intuitive? Did it add unnecessary steps? Did users enjoy the freedom of customization, or did they feel slowed down by an excess of choices? More importantly, does this touchscreen-powered drink dispenser actually deliver on its promise of convenience?

This case study explores those questions, unpacking how technology influences even the smallest moments in our daily lives. From user frustrations to design trade-offs, we’ll examine what happens when customization meets usability—and whether the Freestyle machine strikes the right balance.

Soda machines used to be simple—press your cup against a lever, and your drink poured out instantly. The Coca-Cola Freestyle machine, however, reimagines this process with a high-tech approach, offering hundreds of drink combinations through a touchscreen interface. Instead of a few fixed options, users now scroll, tap, and customize their drinks, choosing flavors, caffeine levels, and calorie preferences.

At its core, the Freestyle machine aims to solve a problem of variety and personalization. No longer limited to whatever happens to be on tap, users can mix and match flavors to create a drink that perfectly suits their taste. But with greater customization comes new interactions—and potential friction. Instead of a quick selection, users must navigate a touchscreen display, browsing through brands and flavors, only to sometimes find their choice grayed out if it’s unavailable. Once a drink is selected, additional options appear, alongside a calorie chart in the top right corner displaying nutritional information. The large activation button in the center changes to show the chosen flavor’s logo and alternates with the text “touch to pour”. Unlike traditional machines, dispensing isn’t automatic—users must tap this button to start. Meanwhile, ice remains a separate process, controlled by a mechanical lever.

To streamline the experience, the interface includes filter categories—such as “low calorie” and “caffeine-free”—to help users find drinks faster. There’s even a QR code that allows mobile interaction, letting users pre-select their drinks. While these features promise a personalized and modern soda experience, they also pose a question: Does this machine truly enhance convenience, or does it overcomplicate a task that used to be effortless?

The Freestyle machine introduces new choices, but also new challenges. While its interface promises a modern, intuitive experience, the reality isn’t always so seamless. A well-designed system should make interactions feel effortless—but does this one? The best way to understand is to watch it in action.

So, I stepped back and observed. How do users interact with the machine? Do they breeze through the options, or do they hesitate? What moments cause frustration? To dig deeper, I interviewed three students—Astrid, Kamryn, and Simon—to hear their thoughts firsthand.

Recording Observations

As I wait in line at the soda machine, I started noticing the little moments—someone hesitating before tapping a drink, a quick glance around to check if others were struggling, the inevitable sigh when the ice ran out yet again. The Coca-Cola Freestyle machine promises customization, but does it actually make the experience easier?

To find out, I observed and interviewed three students—Astrid, Kamryn, and Simon—as they used the machine. Each had a different approach, a unique rhythm in how they navigated the interface. Some moved with confidence, knowing exactly what they wanted. Others paused, scanning the options, sometimes backtracking when a flavor was out of stock.

Across these interactions, patterns emerged. Small design choices—some intuitive, others frustrating—shaped the experience in ways that weren’t always obvious at first. Here’s what I noticed.

Key Observations

  • All three users had a clear idea of what drink they wanted before approaching the machine, indicating a pre-existing preference rather than browsing options.
  • Users typically started by getting ice, but a frequent frustration was the machine running out of ice and showing no indication, leading some to wait for ice out of confusion.
  • The touchscreen interface was generally responsive and well-received, though one user noted concerns about its long-term performance based on prior experience with older models.
  • Users found the brand logos and drink categories easy to navigate, but some were frustrated when flavors were out of stock, requiring extra steps to find an alternative.
  • A common point of confusion was distinguishing between the ice dispenser and drink dispenser, especially for users familiar with traditional push-lever machines.
  • Some users had to pause or backtrack due to mistaken selections, particularly because similar drink variations (e.g., different Minute Maid flavors) were grouped in a way that could be visually confusing.
  • Users mentioned that the machine was generally intuitive but required a small learning curve, especially for first-time users unfamiliar with digital soda dispensers.
  • The machine’s physical design may present accessibility challenges—shorter users might struggle to reach higher buttons, and colorblind users might find it difficult to differentiate grayed-out (unavailable) options.
  • Some users found the touchscreen too bright, especially when standing close to it, which could be uncomfortable in certain lighting conditions.

Interview Summary

1. How do you typically use the soda machine? All three knew their drink beforehand. Astrid and Kamryn grabbed ice first, then selected their drink, while Simon skipped ice and went straight to his Aha sparkling water.

2. What were your expectations before using the machine? They expected slower touch response but were surprised by its speed. Simon assumed drinks would pour immediately after selection.

3. Was anything surprising about the interface? Astrid was frustrated by ice spilling unpredictably. Kamryn liked the vibration feedback, and Simon found the separate physical pour button unnecessary.

4. How straightforward is the process of selecting a drink? Brand logos made navigation easy, but out-of-stock flavors caused extra steps. Simon found the grouping of drinks confusing.

5. Were there any moments of confusion? Simon struggled the first time with ice dispensing and pouring. Kamryn noted that new users might try pressing their cup against the machine, like with manual dispensers.

6. Have you ever found yourself making an unintended selection?" Astrid sometimes misclicked due to similar branding. Kamryn noticed leftover liquid from previous drinks could be misleading.

7. How responsive is the touchscreen? The new machine was fast, but Astrid and Kamryn had dealt with lag on older models. Simon found the reset time between users annoying.

8.Can you estimate how long your interaction with the machine takes? About 30 seconds once at the machine, but waiting in line adds time.

9. What aspects of the interface were most surprising or unexpected? No indication when running out of ice was a shared complaint. Kamryn found reselecting flavors annoying, and Simon mentioned occasional screen freezes.

10. What differences do you notice between this and manual dispensers? Manual dispensers are faster, but the Freestyle machine offers more variety. Kamryn also noted that manual dispensers feel more hygienic.

11. Have you seen others struggle? Ice issues were common. Simon saw users repeatedly pressing buttons, thinking it would speed things up.

12. What could improve the experience? Astrid suggested a hybrid dispensing option like traditional machines. Kamryn wanted better drink organization, and Simon felt the pour button should be on-screen.

13. Do you prefer this over manual dispensers? Astrid prefers manual dispensers for speed. Kamryn likes the variety. Simon finds it less efficient but appreciates the selection.

With our observations and interviews complete, we now have a clearer picture of how users interact with the interface—where they struggle, what they appreciate, and the patterns in their behavior. But raw observations alone don’t tell the full story. To bridge the gap between individual user experiences and broader design insights, we turn to personas. Personas help us distill our findings into representative user archetypes, capturing their goals, frustrations, and expectations in a structured way. By creating empathy maps, we can visualize not just what users do, but also what they think, feel, and say while engaging with the interface. This step moves us from data collection to deeper understanding, setting the stage for effective design improvements.

Personas

From our observations, two distinct user archetypes emerged: Time-Conscious Taylor and Exploratory Lorie. Each persona represents a different approach to using the Coca-Cola Freestyle machine, highlighting unique motivations, frustrations, and interactions with the interface.

Time-Conscious Taylor is busy student who values efficiency above all else. They approach the machine with a clear drink preference, expecting a quick, seamless experience. When faced with unavailable flavors or the extra steps of digital selection, they grow impatient. Observations show that users like Taylor often pause or express frustration when a desired flavor is unavailable.

Users like Exploratory Lorie tend to spend more time browsing flavors, showing engagement with the customization options. They like experimenting with different flavors and appreciate the freedom of choice the machine provides. However, they often feel self-conscious when scrolling through options while others wait behind them, and they find the lack of certain flavors disappointing.

Now that we’ve got our personas, it’s time to bring them to life. Personas help us understand different types of users, but storyboarding lets us see their experience play out step by step. It’s one thing to say that someone values efficiency—it’s another to actually map out what happens when they’re trying to grab a quick drink and hit an unexpected roadblock. For this section, we’ll follow Time-Conscious Taylor, the kind of person who knows exactly what they want and expects to get it fast. Taylor isn’t here to explore new flavors or debate between Cherry Coke and Vanilla Sprite—they just want to fill their cup and move on. But the Coca-Cola Freestyle machine? It has other plans. Between extra taps, out-of-stock flavors, and an interface that demands more attention than a simple push lever, Taylor’s quick stop at the soda machine isn’t as smooth as they hoped.

By walking through their experience, we can pinpoint where the design helps, where it slows things down, and how it could better serve users who just want to grab a drink and go.

At first glance, the Coca-Cola Freestyle machine seems like a win—hundreds of drink options, sleek touchscreen controls, and a modern interface. But for students like Taylor, who are in a rush, the reality is more complicated. Ice shortages, out-of-stock flavors, and extra steps can turn a simple task into an unexpected hassle.

From observing users like Astrid, Kamryn, and Simon, a clear pattern emerged: people love the variety, but they miss the speed and simplicity of traditional dispensers. Some, like Exploratory Lorie, enjoy the customization and are willing to take their time. Others, like Time-Conscious Taylor, just want to grab their drink and go.

And that’s the trade-off: more choices, but more friction. Where a manual dispenser is instant, the Freestyle machine requires extra steps—steps that might be exciting for some but frustrating for others. For users unfamiliar with touchscreen interfaces, the learning curve is real, while those in a hurry feel the pressure of the growing line behind them.

So, does this machine actually improve the experience? It depends on the user. For those who value variety, it’s a dream. For those short on time, it’s another roadblock. The challenge now is designing an experience that balances customization with efficiency—because sometimes, all people really want is a cold drink, without the wait.
